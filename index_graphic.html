Logo Design

Role: Graphic Designer

Expectations: Customer wants original yellow color and Lil House of Donuts name.

The customer was an employee for Lil Orbits Donut Shop who had decided to branch off and start their own local donut shop. They wanted to keep to the original yellow color used at Lil Orbits. With no additional guidance, the rest of the design was up to my creative license.

I began by looking up House of Donuts and found an existing “Original House of Donuts” and took inspiration from their logo. I enjoyed the House shape that was integrated with the company name. The main difference I wanted to give the logo was more simplistic straight lines balancing the cartoonish fun donut- the emphasis being the delicious- mouthwatering donut to draw people's attention.

I maintained the yellow background from 'Lil Orbits' for the customer. The main draw is an abundance of pink to accent the feel of the yellow with a hint of blue to make the donut pop out towards the viewer.

After the first iteration of the Lil House of Donuts logo, I went back into it and defined everything more with blue to really emphasize the company name and accent the donut a little more.

The logo was to be displayed in the center of the Alderwood mall food court, so by adding the blue, I gave it a more stand-out and attractive view to draw the customer in among the other vendors.

To demonstrate how the logo would look, I made a mock-up photo for the customer.

Beer of Perfection

Role: Product Designer, UX Researcher, and Graphic Designer

Expectations: Package a beer with great convenience and taste.

This product concept was my submission for my final in Production Design at Eastern Washington University, a cumulation of my effort across the entire quarter. The project goal was to design both a vessel for beer as well as packaging, logos, and branding surrounding the container. To properly research my product and packaging design I thought I would start at the source - I took a trip to some local breweries. I toured five facilities, from brewing and canning to labeling and marketing, including a one on one Q and A with several brewery owners. After an additional educational trip to Total Wine to look at the variety of labeling and packaging, I took pictures, sketched ideas, and took notes to guide my direction.

I decided on a reusable glass jar with replaceable lids similar to typical cans. This would allow consumers the ability to refill with any beer at any brewhouse without having to commit to the full volume of a growler. The glass achieves a few things; it is the smoothest and least reactive material, allowing for washing and reuse while maintaining the taste and integrity of each new beer, further the glass has a certain appealing texture in the hand that gives the consumer tactile satisfaction. The decision for reusable metal pop-tops is also multifaceted; they are cheap, easy to mass-produce, and recyclable while still granting the customer the satisfying 'CSHH' of cracking open a cold one.

Once I knew the direction the beer packaging was going, I started developing the prototype. This process took most of my time as iteration after iteration there was always something more to improve upon. Whether a better design that didn't require adhesives or a more comfortable handle, I recognized that usability also needed to be factored just as much as design. One of the main challenges that I faced was designing a lock hinge for the bottom of the box that would hold the weight of the beer. My solution was to use origami paper folding techniques to enhance strength.

One of the goals for my packaging design was to reflect both the utility and practicality of the product as well as reflect the draft-brewery capital of the US and my home – the Pacific Northwest. I drew inspiration for the packaging from my surroundings - to reflect the beauty of the Pacific Northwest.

The rugged high peaks, the blowing evergreens, and the abundance of majestic flowing water.

I wanted it to stand out from the more common black and brown color schemes, I chose a bold blue to match the cool environment. Through my research about why beer containers were designed to block most light, I discovered that the light from the sun could chemically damage the beer, degrading the taste and quality. The blue design of the packaging and container not only adds to the aesthetic, but blue is one of the most prominent wavelengths of light the sun produces, allowing my container to block the vast majority of sunlight and keeping the beer fresh.
